01

Genres & Games

What the World Played

Every era of gaming has a signature sound — the blip of an arcade cabinet, the chime of a startup screen, the roar of an online lobby. Behind those sounds is a shifting map of genres: what players loved, and when.

Beginning in 1983, the North American gaming industry suffered a near-fatal crash. An oversaturated console market and fierce competition from home computers pushed several companies into bankruptcy. The savior was the Nintendo Entertainment System, which arrived with a curated library and titles that defined the medium — Super Mario Bros. and The Legend of Zelda among them.

“In 1983 the industry nearly died. Two years later, a single console brought it back — and rewrote what a video game could be.”

In 1989, Nintendo made waves again with the Game Boy, a handheld bundled with the endlessly replayable Tetris. The 1990s brought a flood of arcade-born hits like Street Fighter and Mortal Kombat, and in 1995 Sony's PlayStation took the world by storm — its 3D horsepower birthing series still beloved today, from Resident Evil to Gran Turismo and Tekken.

By the 2000s, cheaper consoles and PCs sent developers chasing international markets. Action-packed franchises like Call of Duty dominated the West, while in Asia a different culture took hold — internet cafés in China and South Korea put high-end gaming within reach of millions.

02

The Console Market

A Global Business

As play went mainstream, gaming became one of the largest entertainment industries on Earth. Nearly 9 billion console games changed hands — but not evenly across the map.

The "console wars" of the 1990s and 2000s — Nintendo versus Sega, then Sony versus Microsoft — turned hardware into a spectator sport of its own. Yet the deeper story lives in where games sold. North America alone accounts for almost half of all units, while Japan's market, though smaller, has long favored its own distinct tastes in RPGs and handhelds.

03

Competitive Gaming

From Bedrooms to Arenas

What began as friendly competition among enthusiasts became a global, billion-dollar spectacle — with professional teams, sold-out stadiums, and prize pools that rival traditional sport.

The roots of esports reach back to 1980's Space Invaders Championship, which drew over 10,000 players in the United States. But competitive gaming truly took shape in the late 1990s with StarCraft in South Korea — the country built professional leagues, dedicated TV channels, and the first true gaming celebrities.

The launch of Twitch in 2011 changed everything, letting anyone watch live matches from anywhere on Earth. League of Legends, Dota 2, and Fortnite pushed the scene to new heights, drawing millions of viewers and prize pools in the tens of millions of dollars.
